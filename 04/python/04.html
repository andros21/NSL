Exercise 04.1¶

The main scope here is to show how initial temperature equilibration is important to achieve accurate MD simulations.

The initial position for all the particles is a critical part of the simulation (for all the three state of matter, gas state first), the main idea is:

  1. start from fcc configuration $r(t)$, with random velocities $v(t)$ rescaled for matching the target equilibration temperature

  2. compute, and evolve with Verlet algo using little number of time steps (where little means lower than steps will be used for real simulation), in this case the evolution is only for initial equilibration purpose. I used $1000$ steps

$$ r_{old}=r(t-dt)= \text{Pbc}\left[r(t) - v(t) * dt\right]$$

  1. write last configuration $r(t)$ and last-last configuration $r(t-dt)$

  2. restart from those configuration files (so no more random velocities)

  3. evolve positions again with little number of time steps, in my case $1000$

  4. refactor the final velocities $v(t)=\alpha\,v(t)$ to obtain the target equilibration temperature

$$ \alpha = \sqrt{\dfrac{3TN_{part}}{\sum v_i^2}} $$
  1. compute ignote $r(t-dt)$ from $r(t)$ and new rescaled $v(t)$
$$ r_{old}=r(t-dt)= \text{Pbc}\left[r(t) - v(t) * dt\right]$$
  1. restart from point 3. until the temperature oscillation are stable, this means that the obtained initial configuration $r(t)$ and $r(t-dt)$ are good enough as starting point. The system is now equilibrated, so the real simulation can start.

Below I plotted the evolution of temperature (for three different state of matter) after $6$ forced equilibrations (velocities rescale) writing temperature to file equil-$state.csv every $10$ time steps. So $1$ temperature every $10$ time steps of the total $1000$ for each of the $6$ equilibrations, means $600$ points temperature plot, plus the $100$ points from the initial random velocities evolution, equal $700$ temperature measures.

As shown, with the solid and liquid state the equilibration was key to reach the target temperature,
with gas state a perfect equilibration is more difficult.

df = dict()
yy = dict()
tt = {"solid": 0.8, "liquid": 1.1, "gas": 1.2}

# load equilibrium oscillation to reach temperature
for state in ["solid", "liquid", "gas"]:
    df[state] = pd.read_csv(f"equil-{state}.out", header=None, names=[state])
    yy[state] = np.ones(df[state].size) * tt[state]

xx = np.arange(1, df["solid"].size+1, 1)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(3, 1, figsize=(11,18));

for ix, state in enumerate(["solid", "liquid", "gas"]):
    df[state][state].plot.line(c='k', lw=0.8, ax=ax[ix]);
    ax[ix].plot(xx, yy[state], 'r-', lw=2);
    ax[ix].set_ylabel(f"T ({state})");

Exercise 04.2 04.3¶

The real simulation part ...

Starting from the $r(t)$ and $r(t-dt)$ after temperature equilibration, evolve the system with high number of steps, I choose $10^5$, save the results for each thermodynamics variables $T$, $K$, $V$, $E$ inside $100$ blocks for computing the moving averages and the uncertainties.

Below for every state of matter, the 4 thermodynamic variables, with blocking methos, using S.I. units.

Theoretically:

  • $E=V+T$
  • $T\propto K$
  • Verlet algo must preserve total energy $E=\text{cost}$

And those proprieties are met very well in liquid and solid, a little less in gas. The gas simulation unfortunately not reached a full equilibration, surely using more simulation steps will reach it.

Again, in gas simulation, precisely in $E$ computation, little problems of decimal truncation have happened during the simulation process, as we can see from the unreal energy step plot.

In all the state of matter the $E$ seems to be not costant, as $V$ or $T$, but those graphs are on different scale. In fact the oscillations of $E$ are always on lower orders compared with energy scales of $T$ and $V$.

    for ch in "TKVE":
        df[state][ch] = pd.read_csv(f"{ch}-{state}-blk.csv",

                                    names=['v','e'])
        df[state][ch] = df[state][ch] * SI[ch]
